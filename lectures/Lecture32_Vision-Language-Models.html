1. Lecture Objectives

This lecture extends the study of Large Language Models to the multimodal setting where visual and linguistic signals are jointly processed. We begin by formalizing the vision-language alignment problem and motivating it with downstream tasks such as image captioning, visual question answering, and zero-shot image classification. We study contrastive pretraining with CLIP as the foundation for aligning image and text representation spaces at scale, and analyze how contrastive objectives produce transferable embeddings. We then examine architectural designs that bridge a pretrained visual encoder with a pretrained language model, including the cross-attention gating approach of Flamingo, the projection-based connector of LLaVA, and the Q-Former bridge of BLIP-2. We study the Visual Transformer (ViT) in detail as the canonical image encoder, discussing how images are patchified and fed into a standard Transformer. We discuss multimodal instruction tuning, which extends LLM-style SFT and RLHF to image-conditioned settings, and survey benchmark tasks used to evaluate VLMs. We close with an analysis of failure modes unique to multimodal models, including hallucination of visual content, modality imbalance, and challenges in spatial and relational reasoning .

2. Motivation and Problem Setting

Human perception is inherently multimodal: we seamlessly integrate visual, auditory, and linguistic information when understanding the world. A Vision-Language Model (VLM) attempts to replicate this cross-modal integration in a machine learning system. Formally, a VLM takes as input an image \(I \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}\) and a text query \(q = (w_1, \ldots, w_T)\), and produces a text response: \[P(y \mid I, q) = \prod_{t} P(y_t \mid I, q, y_1, \ldots, y_{t-1}).\]

Downstream applications include:

  • Image captioning: Generate a natural-language description of an image.

  • Visual Question Answering (VQA): Answer a free-form question about an image.

  • Zero-shot image classification: Rank candidate class names by their compatibility with an image embedding.

  • Optical Character Recognition (OCR) and document understanding: Parse and reason over text embedded in images.

  • Grounding: Localize objects or regions in an image referred to by a textual expression.

  • Multimodal instruction following: Perform multi-step visual tasks described in natural language.

3. The Vision Transformer (ViT)

3.1 Patchification and Embedding

A Vision Transformer (ViT) processes an image by dividing it into a grid of non-overlapping patches of size \(P \times P\) pixels. For an image of resolution \(H \times W\), this yields \(N = (H \cdot W) / P^2\) patches. Each patch \(\mathbf{p}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{P \times P \times C}\) is flattened and linearly projected into a \(d\)-dimensional embedding: \[\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{W}_\text{patch}\, \text{vec}(\mathbf{p}_i) + \mathbf{b},\] where \(\mathbf{W}_\text{patch} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times P^2 C}\). A learnable class token \([\text{CLS}]\) is prepended, and 1D positional embeddings are added to each patch embedding. The resulting sequence of \(N+1\) tokens is processed by a standard Transformer encoder.

3.2 Self-Attention over Image Patches

Because ViT uses the same Transformer attention mechanism as text models, each patch can attend to every other patch in the image. This global receptive field distinguishes ViT from convolutional networks, which have a local receptive field that grows with depth. The \([\text{CLS}]\) token aggregates information from all patches through attention and serves as the global image representation used for downstream tasks.

ViT requires large datasets to achieve strong performance; its lack of inductive biases (spatial locality, translation equivariance) compared to CNNs is a disadvantage on small datasets but an advantage when trained on hundreds of millions of image-text pairs, where the model can learn these biases from data.

3.3 Patch Resolution and Scale

Common ViT configurations include:

  • ViT-B/16: Base model, \(16 \times 16\) patches, 86M parameters.

  • ViT-L/14: Large model, \(14 \times 14\) patches, 307M parameters.

  • ViT-G/14: Giant model, \(14 \times 14\) patches, 1.8B parameters (used in CLIP and Flamingo).

Smaller patch size increases the number of tokens (quadratically increasing attention cost) but preserves finer spatial detail.

4. Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP)

4.1 The Contrastive Objective

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training) learns aligned image and text representations by training on 400 million image-text pairs collected from the internet. The model consists of two encoders:

  • An image encoder \(f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3} \to \mathbb{R}^d\) (a ViT or ResNet).

  • A text encoder \(g_\phi: \mathcal{V}^* \to \mathbb{R}^d\) (a Transformer).

For a batch of \(B\) image-text pairs \(\{(I_i, t_i)\}_{i=1}^B\), image embeddings \(\mathbf{v}_i = f_\theta(I_i) / \|f_\theta(I_i)\|\) and text embeddings \(\mathbf{u}_i = g_\phi(t_i) / \|g_\phi(t_i)\|\) are computed. The similarity matrix \(\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times B}\) has entries: \[S_{ij} = \tau \cdot \mathbf{v}_i^\top \mathbf{u}_j,\] where \(\tau\) is a learned temperature parameter. The training loss is the sum of two symmetric cross-entropy terms: \[\mathcal{L}_\text{CLIP} = -\frac{1}{2B}\sum_{i=1}^B \left[\log \frac{e^{S_{ii}}}{\sum_j e^{S_{ij}}} + \log \frac{e^{S_{ii}}}{\sum_j e^{S_{ji}}}\right].\] Each term treats the matching pair \((I_i, t_i)\) as a positive and all other \(B-1\) pairs as negatives. Training pushes matched image-text embeddings together and unmatched embeddings apart in the shared \(d\)-dimensional space.

4.2 Zero-Shot Classification with CLIP

After training, CLIP can classify images into arbitrary categories without any task-specific training. Given a set of class names \(\{c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_K\}\), text prompts are constructed (e.g., “a photo of a {class}”) and encoded with the text encoder. An image is then classified to the class with highest cosine similarity: \[\hat{y} = \arg\max_{k} \mathbf{v}^\top \mathbf{u}_{c_k}.\] On ImageNet, CLIP achieves accuracy comparable to supervised ResNet-50 without any labeled training examples. This demonstrates that the joint visual-linguistic embedding space generalizes well to novel classification tasks.

4.3 CLIP as a Visual Backbone

Because CLIP visual encoders are trained on diverse image-text pairs, they produce rich, transferable image representations. Modern VLMs almost universally use a pretrained CLIP ViT as the visual backbone, fine-tuning it lightly or keeping it frozen while training the language model interface.

5. Architectures for Vision-Language Models

A core design decision in building a VLM is how to connect the visual encoder to the language model. Three main architectural patterns have emerged.

5.1 Cross-Attention Gating: Flamingo

Flamingo inserts gated cross-attention layers between every layer of a frozen, pretrained language model. Each cross-attention layer allows the language model’s hidden states \(\mathbf{H}\) to attend over visual features \(\mathbf{V}\) extracted by a frozen ViT: \[\mathbf{H}' = \mathbf{H} + \tanh(\alpha) \cdot \text{CrossAttention}(\mathbf{H}, \mathbf{V}, \mathbf{V}),\] where \(\alpha\) is a learnable scalar initialized to 0, ensuring that training begins with the language model unchanged. A Perceiver Resampler module compresses the potentially large set of visual tokens into a fixed number of learned query tokens, making the approach efficient for high-resolution images and videos.

Flamingo demonstrates strong few-shot performance on VQA and captioning benchmarks when provided with visual-linguistic examples in context, analogous to LLM few-shot learning.

5.2 Projection Connector: LLaVA

LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant) takes a simpler approach: a linear projection (or small MLP) maps visual features from the CLIP encoder directly into the token embedding space of a pretrained language model (e.g., LLaMA or Vicuna): \[\mathbf{H}_v = \mathbf{W}\, f_\theta(I),\] where \(\mathbf{H}_v \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_\text{LLM}}\) are the projected visual tokens. These are concatenated with the text token embeddings and fed as a single sequence into the language model. The LLM is then instruction-tuned on visual instruction-following data using standard SFT.

LLaVA demonstrates that a simple projection layer combined with high-quality multimodal instruction data can produce competitive VLMs with much lower training cost than architecturally complex alternatives.

5.3 Q-Former Bridge: BLIP-2

BLIP-2 introduces a Query Transformer (Q-Former), a lightweight Transformer with \(K\) learnable query tokens that extract information from the frozen visual encoder via cross-attention: \[\mathbf{Q}' = \text{QFormer}(\mathbf{Q}, f_\theta(I)),\] where \(\mathbf{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}\) are the learnable queries and \(f_\theta(I)\) are the frozen image features. The \(K\) output vectors (typically \(K = 32\), much smaller than the \(N\) patch tokens) are projected into the LLM embedding space and prepended to the text tokens.

The Q-Former acts as an information bottleneck: it forces the \(K\) queries to distill the most task-relevant visual information from the image features. This compression reduces the number of visual tokens fed into the expensive LLM, lowering computational cost while retaining key visual details.

6. Multimodal Instruction Tuning

Pre-trained VLMs that are only trained for captioning or image-text matching do not follow free-form instructions well. Multimodal instruction tuning fine-tunes the VLM on datasets of the form: \[(\text{image}, \text{instruction}, \text{response}),\] using the same SFT objective as for LLMs. Data is typically generated by prompting a capable LLM (e.g., GPT-4) with image captions and region descriptions to produce diverse question-answer pairs that require visual reasoning. Examples include:

  • “Describe the relationship between the two people in the image.”

  • “What is unusual about this scene?”

  • “How many objects of the same color as the chair are in the image?”

LLaVA-Instruct-158K is a widely-used dataset of 158K such examples. Models fine-tuned on multimodal instruction data can engage in multi-turn visual conversations and perform complex reasoning over images.

6.1 RLHF for VLMs

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback can be applied to VLMs in the same way as for LLMs, with the reward model conditioned on both the image and the text response. This addresses failure modes specific to vision-language settings, such as describing objects that are not present in the image (visual hallucination). Multimodal RLHF is an active research direction; recent work shows that preference feedback on visual outputs reduces hallucination rates substantially.

7. Evaluation Benchmarks

7.1 Visual Question Answering

VQA v2 is a standard benchmark with 265K question-answer pairs over 200K images. Questions require recognizing objects, counting, reading text, and spatial reasoning. Performance is measured as accuracy over a pool of 10 human-collected answers per question. State-of-the-art VLMs achieve 70–85% on VQA v2.

GQA (Graph Question Answering) provides compositional visual questions over structured scene graphs, testing whether models can perform multi-step reasoning over object attributes and relations.

7.2 Image Captioning

COCO Captions is evaluated with BLEU, METEOR, ROUGE, CIDEr, and SPICE metrics that measure the n-gram overlap and semantic similarity between generated and reference captions. BLEU-4 and CIDEr are most commonly reported. However, these metrics correlate imperfectly with human judgment; recent work uses LLM-based evaluators.

7.3 Multimodal Benchmarks

  • MMBench / MME: Comprehensive evaluations of VLM capabilities across perception, cognition, reasoning, and hallucination.

  • ScienceQA: Multi-choice science questions with diagrams, testing scientific reasoning.

  • TextVQA / DocVQA: Questions about text appearing in natural images and scanned documents.

  • POPE: Probing Object Hallucination Evaluation — determines whether a VLM hallucinates objects not present in the image.

8. Failure Modes and Limitations

8.1 Visual Hallucination

The most salient failure mode of VLMs is visual hallucination: generating text that is fluent and contextually plausible but describes visual content not present in the image. This occurs because the language model component has strong textual priors from LLM pretraining that can override visual evidence. For example, a model trained on many images of kitchens may describe a microwave in an image even when none is present.

Mitigation strategies include: (1) object-level grounding supervision, which requires the model to justify descriptions with detected regions; (2) RLHF with hallucination-sensitive reward signals; and (3) chain-of-thought prompting that forces explicit reference to image evidence.

8.2 Modality Imbalance

During multimodal fine-tuning, the language model component often dominates because its weights are pre-trained on far more data than the visual component. The model may answer visual questions using only textual priors without truly processing the image. Diagnosing modality imbalance involves testing model performance when the image is replaced with a blank or random image.

8.3 Spatial and Relational Reasoning

VLMs trained primarily on captioning and VQA tend to perform poorly on tasks requiring precise spatial reasoning (“Is the red box to the left or right of the blue circle?”), counting, and compositional attribute binding (“What is the color of the largest object?”). These limitations reflect the fact that patch-based Transformer encoders aggregate spatial information through attention, losing explicit geometric structure.

8.4 Context Length and Resolution

High-resolution images produce many patch tokens, quickly filling the context window of the language model. Techniques such as dynamic resolution (splitting images into tiles), token merging, and Q-Former compression help manage context length at the cost of fine-grained detail.

9. Summary

Vision-Language Models combine a pretrained visual encoder (typically a ViT trained with CLIP-style contrastive objectives) with a pretrained language model through an architectural connector (projection, cross-attention gating, or Q-Former). CLIP provides a strong foundation by aligning image and text representations in a shared embedding space via large-scale contrastive pretraining. Architectural choices in connecting modalities involve a trade-off between expressivity, computational cost, and the degree of joint fine-tuning. Multimodal instruction tuning adapts the pretrained VLM to follow diverse visual instructions, and RLHF reduces hallucination. Key open challenges include visual hallucination, spatial reasoning, modality imbalance, and scaling to higher resolutions and longer contexts.
