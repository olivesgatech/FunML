Frozen Base Weight W and Trainable Factors B, A

B and A are the only trainable parameters. W stays frozen. During fine-tuning, gradients only flow through B and A. The base model W is preserved and shared across many LoRA adapters - one per downstream task.

Effective Update: Delta W = (alpha / r) B A   and   W + Delta W

Initialization (LoRA paper): A is drawn from a small Gaussian (here with tiny noise so it is visible), and B is initialized near zero. This guarantees Delta W approx 0 at the start of training, so the model behaves identically to the pretrained one before any updates are learned.

Real-world example: Llama-2 7B has ~7e9 parameters. With LoRA r=8 applied to attention projections, only ~4M parameters get trained (~0.06%). This makes fine-tuning feasible on a single consumer GPU and lets you store dozens of task-specific adapters in megabytes instead of tens of gigabytes per checkpoint.

Rank-r Approximation of a Target Update Delta W_true

The target Delta W_true is generated from a random matrix with a decaying singular-value spectrum (typical of weight updates). The rank-r reconstruction is the truncated SVD: U[:, :r] diag(s[:r]) V[:, :r]^T. The residual shows what the rank-r LoRA factorization cannot represent.

Reconstruction Error vs Rank r

Higher r captures more of the target update but costs more parameters. Empirically r = 8 to 16 is often sufficient for LLM fine-tuning - the intrinsic rank of task-specific updates tends to be very low (Hu et al., 2021).
