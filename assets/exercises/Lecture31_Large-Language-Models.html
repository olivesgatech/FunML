Exercises

  1. What is the main objective of a language model? Answer: To estimate the probability of a sequence of tokens. Explanation: A language model assigns probabilities to sequences by modeling \[p(y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n),\] which is typically factorized into conditional next-token predictions using the chain rule.

  2. Why is the chain rule used in language modeling? Answer: It decomposes a complex joint distribution into simpler conditional probabilities. Explanation: Instead of modeling the full sequence probability directly, the chain rule allows the model to predict one token at a time: \[p(y_1,\dots,y_n)=\prod_{t=1}^{n} p(y_t \mid y_{<t}),\] making the problem tractable.

  3. What is the key limitation of \(n\)-gram models? Answer: They rely on a fixed-size context window. Explanation: \(n\)-gram models assume that the next token depends only on the previous \(n-1\) tokens, which prevents them from capturing long-range dependencies in sequences.

  4. Why are tokens preferred over words or characters in modern LLMs? Answer: Tokens balance vocabulary size and sequence length. Explanation: Word-level models have large vocabularies, while character-level models produce long sequences. Subword tokens reuse common patterns and improve efficiency and generalization.

  5. What is a foundation model? Answer: A large pretrained model that can be adapted to many downstream tasks. Explanation: Unlike task-specific models, foundation models are trained on broad data and can generalize across tasks through prompting or fine-tuning.

  6. Why is pretraining alone not sufficient for LLMs? Answer: Pretrained models may produce unaligned or undesirable outputs. Explanation: While pretraining captures language structure, it does not ensure that outputs follow user intent or human preferences, motivating additional stages such as instruction tuning and alignment.

  7. What is the goal of instruction tuning? Answer: To teach the model to follow task-specific instructions. Explanation: Instruction tuning exposes the model to a variety of prompts and tasks, enabling it to generalize to new instructions at inference time.

  8. What is the purpose of alignment tuning? Answer: To align model outputs with human preferences. Explanation: Alignment uses human feedback to train a reward model and optimize the language model so that it produces helpful, harmless, and appropriate responses.

  9. How does LoRA improve fine-tuning efficiency? Answer: By learning low-rank updates instead of full parameter updates. Explanation: LoRA parameterizes weight updates as \[\Delta W = BA,\] which significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters compared to updating the full weight matrix.

  10. What does perplexity measure in language modeling? Answer: How well a model predicts a sequence of tokens. Explanation: Perplexity is the exponential of the average negative log-likelihood per token. Lower perplexity indicates better predictive performance.

  11. Why are BLEU and ROUGE limited as evaluation metrics? Answer: They rely on surface-level overlap and may not capture semantic correctness.

    Explanation: BLEU and ROUGE measure n-gram overlap with reference text, but multiple valid outputs may exist. They do not directly evaluate meaning, reasoning, or factual correctness.

  12. Question: Why do decoder-only Transformer models dominate modern LLMs?

    Solution: Decoder-only models directly implement autoregressive next-token prediction, which aligns naturally with text generation. They are also efficient to train at scale, support flexible prompting, and exhibit strong generalization across tasks.

  13. Question: Can a model have low perplexity but still produce poor outputs? Explain.

    Solution: Yes. Perplexity measures next-token prediction accuracy, not usefulness or correctness of generated outputs. A model may assign high probability to fluent but incorrect or irrelevant text, highlighting the gap between intrinsic metrics and real-world performance.

  14. Question: Why is scaling alone not sufficient to guarantee strong LLM performance?

    Solution: While increasing model size, data, and compute improves performance, outcomes also depend on data quality, architecture, and training strategies. Poor data or suboptimal training can limit performance even at large scale.

  15. What is transfer learning in the context of large language models? Answer: Transfer learning is the process of reusing a pretrained model for new tasks or domains. Explanation: A model trained on large-scale data can transfer its learned representations to downstream tasks, reducing the need for task-specific training from scratch.

  16. What is the difference between fine-tuning and prompting? Answer: Fine-tuning updates model parameters, while prompting does not. Explanation: Fine-tuning adapts the model using labeled data, whereas prompting conditions the model’s behavior through input context without modifying its parameters.

  17. What is domain adaptation and why is it important? Answer: Domain adaptation is the process of adapting a pretrained model to a new data distribution. Explanation: It allows models to perform well in specialized domains such as medical or legal text, where the data distribution differs from general pretraining data.

  18. What is the main difference between RLHF and DPO? Answer: RLHF uses a reward model and reinforcement learning, while DPO directly optimizes preferences without an explicit reward model. Explanation: In RLHF, a separate reward model \(r_\phi(x,y)\) is trained from human preferences, and the policy is optimized using reinforcement learning (e.g., PPO). In contrast, DPO eliminates the reward model and directly updates the language model using preference pairs: \[(y^+, y^-)\] by increasing the probability of preferred outputs. This makes DPO simpler and more stable, while RLHF is more flexible but computationally more complex.
