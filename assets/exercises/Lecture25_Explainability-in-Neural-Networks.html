Exercises

  1. Question: Compare occlusion-based saliency and gradient-based saliency in terms of faithfulness and computational cost.

    Solution: Occlusion-based saliency measures importance by directly perturbing the input and observing changes in the prediction, making it causally grounded but computationally expensive due to many forward passes. Gradient-based saliency is efficient (single forward/backward pass) but provides only a local linear approximation of importance.

  2. Question: Explain why early convolutional layers and fully connected layers are both unsuitable for producing meaningful localization maps in Grad-CAM.

    Solution: Early layers retain spatial resolution but encode only low-level features (edges/textures), lacking semantic meaning. Fully connected layers capture high-level semantics but discard spatial structure. Grad-CAM therefore uses the last convolutional layer as a compromise.

  3. Question: How do Vanilla Backpropagation, Deconvnet, and Guided Backpropagation differ in how they handle gradients through ReLU?

    Solution: Vanilla BP blocks gradients where forward activations were negative. Deconvnet blocks negative gradients during the backward pass. Guided BP blocks gradients unless both the forward activation and backward gradient are positive, producing sharper maps.

  4. Question: Why does Guided Backpropagation lack class discriminativity?

    Solution: It primarily highlights generic edges and textures based on gradient flow, rather than weighting feature maps by class-specific importance. As a result, maps for different classes often appear similar.

  5. Question: In Grad-CAM, what is the interpretation of the weight \[\alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z} \sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A_{ij}^k} ?\]

    Solution: It measures how strongly feature map \(k\) influences the target class score \(y^c\) on average across spatial locations. These weights determine how much each feature map contributes to the final class activation map.

  6. Question: Why is a ReLU applied after computing \[L_{\text{Grad-CAM}}^c = \sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k ?\]

    Solution: ReLU removes negative contributions, retaining only features that positively support the target class. This improves interpretability by focusing the heatmap on regions that provide evidence for the class rather than against it.

  7. Question: Why is LIME considered a local explanation method rather than a global explanation method?

    Solution: LIME approximates the behavior of a complex model only in the neighborhood of a specific input by fitting a simple surrogate model to perturbed samples. Because this approximation is valid only near that instance, it does not necessarily reflect the model’s behavior across the entire dataset. Therefore, LIME provides local explanations rather than global interpretability.

  8. Question: Why does LIME generate perturbed samples when constructing explanations?

    Solution: LIME generates perturbed samples to observe how small changes in the input affect the model’s prediction. By analyzing how predictions vary with these perturbations, LIME can estimate which features are most influential for that specific prediction. This allows the method to approximate the model’s local decision boundary.

  9. Question: What is the main theoretical advantage of SHAP over LIME?

    Solution: SHAP is based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory, which provide a theoretically grounded way to fairly attribute feature importance. These values satisfy desirable properties such as consistency and additivity. In contrast, LIME relies on locally fitted surrogate models and does not guarantee these theoretical properties.

  10. Question: Why is computing exact SHAP values often computationally expensive?

    Solution: Computing exact Shapley values requires evaluating the contribution of each feature across all possible subsets of features. Since the number of subsets grows exponentially with the number of features, exact computation becomes intractable for high-dimensional data. Practical implementations therefore rely on approximations.

  11. Question: Compare LIME and SHAP in terms of explanation stability.

    Solution: LIME explanations may vary depending on the randomly generated perturbations used to construct the local surrogate model, which can reduce stability. SHAP explanations tend to be more stable because they are derived from a principled attribution framework based on Shapley values, although approximations may still introduce some variability.

  12. Question: When might LIME be preferred over SHAP in practice?

    Solution: LIME may be preferred when computational efficiency is important, since it typically requires fewer computations than SHAP. It may also be preferred when quick local explanations are sufficient and theoretical guarantees are less critical.
