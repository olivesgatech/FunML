Exercises

  1. Why are VLMs modeled using left-to-right probability? Answer: Because they extend LLMs to multimodal settings. Explanation: VLMs follow the same autoregressive principle as LLMs, where tokens are generated sequentially. The difference is that the conditioning context includes both previous text tokens and visual tokens extracted from images.

  2. What is the main difference between CLIP and generative VLMs? Answer: CLIP is contrastive, while generative VLMs are autoregressive. Explanation: CLIP learns aligned embeddings for images and text using a contrastive loss, but does not generate text. Generative VLMs explicitly model \(p(y \mid x)\) and produce outputs token by token.

  3. Why is multimodal fusion difficult? Answer: Because modalities are heterogeneous and often misaligned. Explanation: Images and text differ in structure, scale, and representation. Additionally, corresponding information may be incomplete, noisy, or temporally misaligned, making it difficult to learn a shared representation.

  4. Why are ViT-based encoders preferred in modern VLMs? Answer: Because they produce token-based representations. Explanation: Vision Transformers convert images into sequences of tokens, which align naturally with Transformer-based language models. This simplifies multimodal fusion compared to CNN-based feature maps.

  5. What makes evaluation harder in VLMs than in LLMs? Answer: Evaluation must consider both language quality and visual grounding. Explanation: Unlike LLMs, VLMs must ensure that outputs are not only fluent but also consistent with the visual input. This introduces additional challenges such as grounding, hallucination detection, and multimodal alignment.

  6. What is the difference between early fusion and late fusion? Answer: Early fusion combines modalities at the token level, while late fusion combines them after separate encoding. Explanation: In early fusion, visual and textual tokens are processed together in a shared sequence model, enabling rich cross-modal interactions from the start. In late fusion, each modality is encoded separately and combined later through mechanisms such as cross-attention, which improves modularity but may limit early interaction.

  7. What is the purpose of alignment tuning in VLMs? Answer: To ensure outputs are helpful, safe, and aligned with human preferences. Explanation: Alignment tuning uses human feedback or preference data to refine model behavior beyond instruction following. Methods such as RLHF and DPO help ensure that responses are not only correct and grounded in visual input but also appropriate and aligned with user expectations.
